# Episode 6 — The Worms

- Season: 1 · The Critters
- Runtime target: ~7 min (~970 words)
- Voice: en-US-AndrewMultilingualNeural

## Cold open

[SHOT: topdown grass — bare soil between grass towers, nothing apparently moving]

Every episode so far has looked up — at wings, webs, and towers of grass. This one looks down, because the animal that makes all of that possible spends its whole life where nobody is watching.

[SHOT: closeup worm — macro on soil grains shifting, then a smooth body sliding through frame]

No eyes. No legs. No lungs. By every measure this series has celebrated, the earthworm should be nothing at all.

[SHOT: track worm — low and close, following the slow ripple of its body across the ground]

And yet the garden, quite literally, rests on it.

## What it actually is

[SHOT: closeup worm — extreme macro on the body, ring after ring segmenting its length]

Start with what it is. An earthworm is not a snake, not a grub, not a root. It is an annelid — a segmented worm — and those rings running down its body are true divisions, each carrying its own muscles, nerves, and tiny bristles that grip the soil like cleats and drive it forward.

[SHOT: orbit worm — circle the worm as it slithers, the S-curve of its path drawn in the soil]

It breathes without a single lung. Oxygen passes straight through its skin — which is why that skin has to stay damp, and why a worm caught out dry on a hot path is a worm in real danger.

[SHOT: low worm — soil level, the worm approaching camera, huge and unhurried]

And it is both sexes at once. Every earthworm is male and female together. Two still meet to trade, but neither is simply mother or father. Even reproduction, down here, is a kind of cooperation.

## Three trades, three depths

[SHOT: cutaway grass — sweep across the grass ocean above, then dive back to the soil]

There is no single kind of earthworm; there are thousands. Science sorts them by where they work. Some live in the loose litter right at the surface, feeding on fallen leaves and barely burrowing at all.

[SHOT: closeup worm — a worm threading horizontally through dark topsoil]

Others spend their lives in the topsoil, tunneling sideways, eating their way through the ground itself and rarely coming up.

[SHOT: closeup worm — night surface: the front half reaching out from a deep vertical burrow]

And a few — the deep-burrowers, the nightcrawlers — dig straight down in single vertical shafts that can run astonishingly deep. At night they reach out from the mouth of the burrow and haul dead leaves down into the earth to eat. Three trades, three depths, one soil rebuilt from the top all the way down.

## The engine of the soil

[SHOT: closeup worm — the worm passes soil through itself, castings left behind]

Here is the mechanism the whole garden depends on. The worm eats the ground. It swallows soil and dead matter, grinds and digests what was once living in it, and leaves behind castings — droppings richer in the nutrients plants crave than the dirt that went in. A worm is a fertilizer factory that walks.

[SHOT: track worm — following the open channel of a burrow down through the soil]

And the tunnels matter as much as the castings. Every burrow is a channel for air and for water. Rain sinks down them instead of running off. Roots follow them into ground that was once too hard to enter. The soil itself begins to breathe. Loosened, drained, and fed — this is earth the worm has quietly made fertile.

[SHOT: wide field — the full meadow: grass, flowers, trees, all in one frame]

Multiply that by every worm beneath this meadow, and the towers above stand upon it — the grass, the flower fields, the great trees, all rooted in ground that blind engineers turn over and over, every hour the soil is soft.

## Rain, and the price of surfacing

[SHOT: cutaway rain — the sky darkens; rain streaks hammer the canopy above]

The worm's world has its own weather report, and rain writes it. Waterlogged ground can leave the burrows short of oxygen, and a worm that cannot get enough air through the soaked earth must do the most dangerous thing it ever does. It surfaces.

[SHOT: topdown worm — from above, worms on wet open ground between the grass stems]

Out on the open floor it has no defense at all. And it is far from alone in knowing that.

[SHOT: cutaway birds — the flock banks sharply and descends toward the field]

For the birds, a wet morning is market day — a glistening ribbon of protein laid out in the open. For the moles below and the beetles alongside, the worm is food as well. It feeds a great deal of the garden without ever intending to.

## The animal Darwin studied last

[SHOT: closeup worm — extreme macro on the head end, featureless, pressing forward through soil]

The idea that this animal is beneath notice is an old mistake, and a famous one corrected it. The last book Charles Darwin ever wrote was not about finches or islands. It was about earthworms — a lifetime's argument that these small tillers, laboring unseen across the ages, had slowly remade the very surface of the land.

[SHOT: cutaway tree — a shaded forest floor, thick with fallen leaves]

They are so good at their work, in fact, that they can become too much of a good thing. In some northern forests that grew up with no earthworms at all, introduced worms strip the leaf litter so fast the whole woodland floor is transformed — proof, in reverse, of just how powerfully they rewrite the ground.

[SHOT: topdown worm — dawn: castings dotting the soil, small monuments to the night's work]

By morning, the only evidence is the castings — small coiled heaps of processed earth. The residents step over them without a glance. Every one is a receipt: the foundation was serviced again last night, and the bill, as always, was nothing.

## The unseen foundation

[SHOT: crane field — slow rise from soil level up past grass, flowers, treetops, to open sky]

Hold both in mind at once — the shining meadow, and the blind engine beneath it. The Gardener planted this world's surface. The worms, one swallowed grain at a time, keep its foundation alive.

[SHOT: closeup flower — a bloom sways; a deep hum grows on the soundtrack]

Above that dark kingdom, the meadow is advertising — color, scent, and geometry, all of it aimed at one buzzing guild of customers whose daily rounds keep the flowers in business.

[SHOT: track bee — a bumblebee barrels past, heavy and determined, toward the blooms]

Fuzzy, industrious, and gloriously indelicate. Next: the bumblebees.
